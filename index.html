Spatify

Spatify is a proof-of-concept for an app similar to Groupon or Yelp but with emphasis on mutually beneficial deals. Empty seats in salons and spas generate zero revenue so why not offer them in a firesale format at a heavy discount? Consumers looking for something to do can make an immediate appointment for much less than they would pay normally. This app runs using HTML, CSS with Materialize, and JavaScript on the front end. The back end uses Node.js, Express, MySQL with Sequelize, Node Mailer, and later.js

The Cave Pearl Project

The Cave Pearl Project is a project aimed at replacing expensive commercial environmental sensors with open-source, microcontroller-powered, data logging platforms. My participation in this project was developing a lux (light intensity) sensor that could be deployed for months at a time deep in flooded caves. In addition to developing the physical build, I wrote the code with the goal of minimal power-usage and maximum sensitivity (in a pitch-black cave there aren't many false positives). This was done in the Arduino IDE using C++. The aim was to deploy these sensors in a cave known as El Hoyo Negro, an important archeological site. There are multiple, supposedly secure, entrances to the cave. With these sensors, we can log the frequency and direction the trespassers come from based off the sensors detecting trace amounts of light given off by their diving flashlights (pitch black cave - a necessity!).

Gravity Model

One of my first projects. A relatively simple Python program using NumPy MatPlotLib and Math modules to run a series of loops over user-generated polygons representing rock layers (strata) in the Earth. The equations used are from Talwani et al., 1959. The effect of the individual layer's density on the overall strength of the gravitational field of the area is calculated for each vertex of each polygon and averaged over the size of the polygon. For example, dense volcanic layers would cause an increase in gravitational force (only noticeable to super-sensitive instrumentation, you're not going to sink into the ground) whereas light, sedimentary layers would have the opposite effect.

Geoscience Work

Thanks for your interest! I was fortunate enough to present my research at several conferences and even be listed as a co-author on an article published in GSA Today. Below you can find summaries of my 2 favorite pieces of work.

Publications "Is the coast toast?" This article offers an easy-to-understand breakdown of upper-level earthquake probability models. Essentially, when modeling you can have "time dependent" and "time independent" models. Time dependent means that the next earthquake depends on when the previous one happened, time independent means earthquakes happen independently of each other. There is also the issue of "clustering" - do earthquakes happen in clusters? Are we in a cluster right now? Using these parameters, you can drastically alter the predicted timeline.

Link

"Earthquakes and Moneyball." This talk, presented at the Geological Society of America 2017 Fall Meeting, expands the discussion of predicting earthquake probabilities by using baseball metaphors as a teaching tool. Is the batter in a hot streak (cluster)? Is it better to look at recent performance versus long-term stats (time dependent versus independent). A similar version of this was presented the American Geophysical Union 2018 Winter Meeting.
